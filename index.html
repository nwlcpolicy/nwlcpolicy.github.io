NATIONAL WOMEN'S LIBERAL COMMISSION — POLICY BULLETIN 2026 Policy Cycle: Resolutions, Ideas & Our Process 26 resolutions. Nine issue clusters. One shared vision for women in Canada. Spring 2026  ·  NWLC Policy Committee  ·  Dr. Maria Omar, WLC BC Policy Chair The National Women's Liberal Commission opens every policy cycle by listening — to members, to data, and to the communities most affected by the decisions made in Ottawa. This edition brings together 26 resolutions submitted for the 2026 cycle, organized across nine issue clusters so you can see where our collective energy is focused. Alongside the formal resolutions, we share the ideas emerging from our Policy Café conversations, and a plain-language overview of how a resolution moves from a single woman's lived experience to a national party position. Good policies get voted in and stay — but only when implementation follows intention. That is the work ahead of us. NWLC RESOLUTION Achieving Healthcare for All Canadians: A 21st Century Restructuring of the Healthcare System Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission  ·  Contact: Judith Quinn Canada's healthcare system was designed for a different era. Today, millions of Canadians lack a family doctor, wait times are among the highest in the developed world, and women — who represent the majority of unpaid caregivers and healthcare workers — shoulder the heaviest burden of a system in crisis. This resolution calls on the Liberal Party to champion a comprehensive restructuring, establishing a Canada Primary Care Act that guarantees every Canadian access to a primary care provider within two years, alongside expanded digital health infrastructure, mental health parity, and long-term care reform. KEY ASKS • Enact a Canada Primary Care Act guaranteeing access to a primary care provider for all Canadians • Invest in digital health infrastructure including national electronic health records and telehealth expansion • Establish mental health parity — equal coverage for mental and physical health under public insurance • Reform long-term care with national standards, inspections, and a dedicated federal funding stream • Increase funding and training for healthcare workers, recognizing women's disproportionate representation in the sector WLC ALBERTA Codifying Reproductive Rights and Access in Canadian Law Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Zuraida Dada Reproductive rights remain uncodified in Canadian law — a gap that leaves access to abortion and reproductive healthcare vulnerable to political shifts at the provincial level. With legislative attacks on reproductive rights accelerating in neighbouring jurisdictions, this resolution calls for federal codification as a non-negotiable foundation of women's equality. KEY ASKS • Pass federal legislation codifying the right to abortion and reproductive healthcare access across all provinces and territories • Establish national funding floors for reproductive health services that provinces cannot reduce • Protect healthcare workers from harassment when providing lawful reproductive services • Include contraception and sexual health education as insured services under provincial health plans, with federal incentives WLC MANITOBA Establishing a Crown Corporation for Pharmaceutical Production and Raw Chemical Synthesis Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson The COVID-19 pandemic exposed Canada's dangerous dependence on foreign pharmaceutical supply chains. Drug shortages — which disproportionately affect women and children — have become routine. This resolution calls for a Canadian pharmaceutical Crown corporation to manufacture essential medicines domestically, ensuring supply security and reducing costs. KEY ASKS • Establish a federal Crown corporation for domestic production of essential pharmaceuticals and raw chemical precursors • Prioritize production of medications disproportionately needed by women — including hormonal therapies, reproductive health medications, and mental health pharmaceuticals • Partner with Canadian universities and biotech sector for research and development • Implement a national drug shortage monitoring and response system WLC BRITISH COLUMBIA Access to Independent Medical Examinations for Victims of Sexual Assaults and Harassment in the Military Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Dr. Maria Omar Victims of sexual assault and harassment within the Canadian Armed Forces are currently examined by military medical personnel — creating an inherent conflict of interest that undermines survivor trust, compromises forensic evidence, and deters reporting. This resolution calls for independent, civilian-conducted medical examinations as a matter of survivor dignity and justice. KEY ASKS • Mandate independent civilian medical examinations for all victims of sexual assault and harassment within the Canadian Armed Forces • Establish trauma-informed protocols for evidence collection and survivor support, co-developed with survivors • Ensure confidentiality and chain of custody for forensic evidence independent of the military chain of command • Fund specialized Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) training for civilian practitioners serving military communities WLC ALBERTA Healing Sexual Violence Survivors Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Ingrid Dandanell Between 30–44% of Canadian women experience sexual violence in their lifetime. Yet healing from that violence — which requires long-term therapy — is not covered under the Canada Health Act unless provided by a physician or in a hospital. Certified counsellors, clinical social workers, and registered psychologists, whose care is often the most appropriate and accessible, are entirely excluded. This resolution closes that gap through a targeted tax credit for survivor therapy costs. KEY ASKS • Create a refundable federal tax credit for mental health treatment costs incurred by survivors of sexual violence, covering certified counsellors, clinical social workers, and registered psychologists • Extend the credit to all survivors regardless of age, with a parent-claimable option for minors • Require certification by a recognized health professional to prevent misuse while ensuring broad access • Recognize that Indigenous, racialized, rural, and 2SLGBTQIA+ survivors face compounded barriers and ensure the credit design reaches these communities • Acknowledge childhood sexual abuse as a primary driver of women's homelessness — treating survivors is housing prevention CLUSTER 2 — GENDER-BASED VIOLENCE & SAFETY When the System Fails Women, We Change the System. "Intimate partner violence kills a woman every six days in Canada. Bailey McCourt was one of them. Her family asked: why did the system let this happen? This resolution is our answer." — WLC BC, Resolution Preamble, 2026 WLC BRITISH COLUMBIA Advancing Bailey's Law and Reforms to Protect Victims of Intimate Partner Violence Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Dr. Maria Omar Bailey McCourt was murdered by her estranged husband — released on $500 bail despite prior convictions. Her death, and the deaths of so many women like her, reveals systemic failures in how Canada handles intimate partner violence: bail too low, monitoring too weak, records too fragmented, and femicide not recognized in law as what it is. This resolution advances "Bailey's Law" — a package of reforms co-developed with Bailey's family, survivors, and Indigenous communities to close these gaps. KEY ASKS • Bail Reform: Amend the Criminal Code to reverse the onus in intimate partner violence cases — accused must prove they pose no risk, with courts required to weigh prior convictions, stalking, and victim statements • GPS Electronic Monitoring: Mandate offender-funded GPS monitoring for high-risk accused, with real-time victim alerts via app — developed with Indigenous and community input • National Registry: Create a PIPEDA-compliant, AI-supported database of repeat intimate partner violence offenders, accessible to police and courts • Recognize Femicide: Amend the Criminal Code to recognize femicide as a distinct offence; presume first-degree murder when a killing follows documented abuse • Allocate $100 million annually for trauma-informed mental health, legal aid, and culturally appropriate shelters NSWLC — NOVA SCOTIA Stop Ruthless Cuts to Women and Gender Equality Canada (WAGE) Sponsor: Nova Scotia Women's Liberal Commission The 81% cut to WAGE's 2025–2028 budget is not a line-item adjustment — it is the dismantling of the infrastructure that funds shelters, victim support agencies, the National Action Plan on Gender-Based Violence, the 2SLGBTQI+ Secretariat, and the gender budgeting analysis that keeps equality commitments accountable. These cuts reverse decades of progress and renege on a pre-election promise: "We will protect these rights, and fight for true equity for women — in health, in the workplace, and in their access to fundamental rights." KEY ASKS • Maintain and increase WAGE funding — reject the proposed 81% cut to the 2025–2028 budget • Recognize that investing in gender equality is fundamental to nation-building, economic growth, and social stability • Honour the National Action Plan to End Gender-Based Violence and all associated funding commitments • Protect the 2SLGBTQI+ Secretariat and gender budgeting infrastructure from austerity cuts • Uphold Canada's international obligations on gender equality and reject austerity measures that threaten hard-won rights WLC ALBERTA Pan-Canadian Framework for Domestic Violence Disclosure Schemes (Clare's Law) Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Zuraida Dada Domestic violence disclosure schemes — known as "Clare's Law" — allow individuals at risk to request verified information about a partner's history of violence. Several provinces have adopted versions of this framework, but uneven implementation means that protection depends on where you live. A survivor seeking information in one province may be protected; a survivor in another may not be. This resolution establishes a national standard. KEY ASKS • Establish a Pan-Canadian Framework for Domestic Violence Disclosure developed collaboratively with provinces, territories, and Indigenous governments • Create a voluntary federal digital portal with shared data and privacy standards enabling consistent access across jurisdictions • Provide conditional federal funding and intergovernmental agreements to encourage full adoption and ensure equitable protection for all Canadians • Ensure the framework is trauma-informed and culturally appropriate, with specific provisions for Indigenous women and newcomers • Support partners and survivors who are still living with or reconciling with abusive partners, recognizing that safety planning is not always linear NWLC RESOLUTION Preventing the Misuse of Non-Disclosure Agreements Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission  ·  Contact: Judith Quinn Non-disclosure agreements were designed to protect trade secrets and intellectual property. They have become a tool for silencing victims. Women are five times more likely than men to sign NDAs related to sexual harassment settlements or pregnancy-related disputes. Racialized and Indigenous workers are disproportionately coerced into signing NDAs, and less likely to make formal complaints. This resolution stops the weaponization of legal instruments against survivors. KEY ASKS • Prohibit NDAs that prevent disclosure of sexual harassment, discrimination, and human rights abuses in federal workplaces and federally regulated sectors • Require that any NDA related to workplace misconduct include a mandatory carve-out allowing disclosure to police, lawyers, and health professionals • Establish independent legal advice requirements before any settlement NDA can be signed in cases involving harassment or abuse • Protect employees who break NDAs signed under duress or coercion from civil and criminal liability • Collect and publish disaggregated data on NDA use by sector, gender, and race to enable evidence-based policy development NWLC RESOLUTION Protecting the Public and Our Institutions from Bad Actors — Whistleblower Reform Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission  ·  Contact: Judith Quinn Whistleblowers are Canada's first line of defence against political and financial wrongdoing. Yet most workers who report wrongdoing in Canada have no legal protection — and current federal legislation actively re-victimizes them while shielding alleged wrongdoers. Canada lags decades behind the 2019 EU Whistleblower Protection Directive. Women who report workplace wrongdoing face gendered retaliation, including career destruction and harassment. This must change. KEY ASKS • Enact legislation modelled on the EU Whistleblower Protection Directive with broader scope, stronger protections, and meaningful enforcement • Establish independent oversight bodies to investigate retaliation complaints outside the employing institution • Reverse the onus of proof — employers must prove that adverse actions against a whistleblower are not retaliatory • Provide criminal and civil immunity for breaking NDAs in order to report public interest wrongdoing • Mandate confidential reporting channels in all federally regulated workplaces, with anonymous submission options CLUSTER 3 — MENTAL HEALTH & INCLUSION Care That Reaches Every Woman. WLC ALBERTA Advancing Culturally Sensitive Mental Health Care in Canada Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Zuraida Dada Canada's mental health system was not designed with cultural diversity in mind. Immigrant women, racialized communities, Indigenous peoples, and newcomers face a system that often misdiagnoses, pathologizes cultural expression, or simply lacks practitioners who understand their context. The result: women who most need care go without it, or are harmed by it. This resolution calls for a national framework for culturally sensitive mental health care — not as an add-on, but as a standard of quality. KEY ASKS • Develop a National Framework for Culturally Safe Mental Health Care co-designed with racialized, Indigenous, and newcomer communities • Fund training requirements in cultural competence and anti-oppression for all federally regulated mental health providers • Increase diversity in mental health workforce through targeted scholarships and international credential recognition • Establish community mental health hubs in partnership with cultural and faith organizations serving underrepresented communities • Require disaggregated data collection on mental health service access by race, gender, immigration status, and disability WLC RESOLUTION Livable, Not Laughable: Transforming the Canada Disability Benefit Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Bill C-22 created the Canada Disability Benefit — but the benefit as implemented leaves recipients below the poverty line. At $200/month, it fails to cover basic necessities in any major Canadian city. Women with disabilities are disproportionately affected, facing compounded barriers of the gender pay gap, caregiving responsibilities, and the additional costs of living with disability. This resolution demands a benefit that is actually livable. KEY ASKS • Increase the Canada Disability Benefit to a livable income standard aligned with the Market Basket Measure, adjusted regionally for cost of living • Remove spousal income clawbacks — disability benefits must support the individual's independence, not penalize those with partners • Index benefits to inflation and disability-specific cost increases, including medical equipment, accessible housing, and specialized supports • Harmonize CDB, CPP-D, and DTC to eliminate benefit cliffs that trap recipients in poverty • Provide dedicated relocation support for persons with disabilities who must move to access critical services or facilities WLC RESOLUTION Protection of the Rights and Safety of Sex Workers Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Sex workers — the majority of whom are women — face systemic violence, criminalization, and exclusion from health and safety protections available to other workers. Canada's current legal framework under the Protection of Communities and Exploited Persons Act criminalizes aspects of sex work in ways that drive it underground, increasing danger and limiting access to justice. This resolution calls for a rights-based approach grounded in evidence, not stigma. KEY ASKS • Repeal provisions of PCEPA that criminalize sex workers and their clients in ways that increase danger, undermine safety planning, and prevent access to justice • Develop policy in full consultation with sex workers, particularly Indigenous and racialized workers who face heightened risks from over-policing • Guarantee sex workers' access to labour protections, healthcare, and justice services without fear of criminalization • Distinguish clearly between consensual adult sex work and trafficking — conflating the two harms both survivors and workers • Fund peer-led support organizations that provide harm reduction, health services, and legal assistance to sex workers CLUSTER 4 — HOUSING Unlocking Land. WLC BRITISH COLUMBIA Faith Land Housing Resolution Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Anne Marie Varner Across Canada, faith communities hold significant parcels of underused or surplus land — land that could house thousands of families. Regulatory barriers, zoning restrictions, and complex partnership frameworks have prevented faith-owned land from contributing to the affordable housing supply, even when faith communities are willing and eager to act. This resolution calls on the federal government to work with faith communities, municipalities, and non-profits to unlock this land for affordable housing development, particularly for women fleeing violence, single-parent families, seniors, and newcomers. KEY ASKS • Create a Faith Land–Affordable Housing Partnership Fund to support development on faith-community-owned surplus land • Streamline federal incentives and tax treatment for faith organizations that partner with non-profits to build affordable housing • Establish a national inventory of faith-owned land with potential for housing development • Prioritize units for women fleeing violence, single-parent families, seniors, and newcomers • Ensure developments include wraparound supports — childcare, community space, culturally appropriate services WLC RESOLUTION Affordable and Sustainable Housing Model Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Canada's housing crisis is not a natural disaster — it is a policy failure. Average home prices in Toronto and Vancouver have risen over 200% since 2010. Homeownership rates for Canadians under 35 have collapsed from 55% to below 35%. Over 20% of urban properties are owned by corporations or non-resident speculators. Women, single-parent families, newcomers, and low-income earners bear the heaviest burden. This resolution demands structural intervention. KEY ASKS • Create a National Affordable Housing Development Fund targeting 500,000 new non-market units over 10 years, prioritizing women-led households, single parents, and newcomers • Implement a vacancy and speculation tax on non-resident-owned and corporation-owned residential properties to redirect investment to homeowners and renters • Reform zoning and permitting processes to accelerate construction timelines and mandate inclusionary zoning requiring affordable units in all new developments • Expand the First Home Savings Account and down payment assistance for households earning under the median income • Develop a housing financialization monitoring system with transparent data on corporate ownership trends and market interventions WLC BRITISH COLUMBIA Supporting Canadian Seniors: Housing, Mobility, and Dignity Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Anne Marie Varner Canada's senior population will represent 25% of the population by 2030 — yet housing policy continues to treat seniors as an afterthought. Sixty percent of seniors delay moving to more appropriate housing because the costs are prohibitive. Foreign ownership has exacerbated an already tight housing supply. Women, who make up the majority of seniors and outlive male partners, face particular vulnerability. This resolution puts seniors' housing security at the centre of national housing policy. KEY ASKS • Urgently address the shortage of senior-appropriate housing units in partnership with provinces, territories, and municipalities • Create financial transition support for seniors moving to smaller, more appropriate accommodations — including moving cost subsidies and portability of property tax deferrals • Restrict foreign ownership of senior housing developments and long-term care facilities to keep them in community hands • Develop a national inventory of senior housing needs disaggregated by gender, income, and geography • Ensure all new affordable housing developments include accessibility standards appropriate for aging-in-place CLUSTER 5 — GOVERNANCE A Democracy That Looks Like Canada. AORRH WLC Electoral Reform — Move to a Proportional Representation System Sponsor: WLC AORRH  ·  Contact: Wendy Morihovitis Under Canada's first-past-the-post system, millions of votes do not elect anyone — and women, Indigenous peoples, and racialized Canadians are consistently underrepresented in legislatures. Proportional representation systems consistently produce more diverse elected bodies, better reflect the range of views held by voters, and reduce the "wasted vote" effect that discourages participation. This resolution calls on the Liberal Party to revisit its commitment to electoral reform and to champion a proportional or mixed-member system through a citizen-led process — modelled in part on the experiences of New Zealand and Germany. KEY ASKS • Convene a Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform with gender-balanced, regionally representative, and Indigenous membership • Commit to a referendum on proportional representation within the first two years of the next mandate, with the Citizens' Assembly recommendation as the preferred option • Adopt gender parity provisions in any new electoral system design • Implement automatic voter registration and expand early and online voting to increase participation, particularly among women, youth, and newcomers NWLC RESOLUTION Veteran Survivors' Pension — Full Benefits for Military Spouses Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Military spouses follow their partners through a minimum of three postings during a career — relocations that make it impossible to build a pension, accumulate CPP contributions, or maintain continuous employment. When a veteran dies, the spouse who sacrificed her own career for Canada's Armed Forces receives just half the military pension and a fraction of CPP. This resolution closes that gap. KEY ASKS • Allow surviving veterans' spouses to receive the full CPP and other pension benefits of the veteran for the remainder of the survivor's life • Recognize military-spouse career disruption as a qualifying factor in survivor benefit calculations • End the reduction of survivor benefits to 50% of the military pension upon the veteran's death • Review the 37.5% / 60% CPP survivor split threshold and align it with the economic reality of military family life WLC RESOLUTION Mitigating the Epidemic of Electoral Online Misinformation Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission  ·  Contact: Sumana Gupta The proliferation of AI-generated electoral misinformation on social media — combined with platforms' persistent unwillingness to moderate it — poses an existential threat to Canada's democratic integrity. Women candidates are disproportionately targeted by online misinformation and harassment campaigns. The 2025 federal election demonstrated that coordinated disinformation can affect electoral outcomes and erode public trust in democratic institutions. KEY ASKS • Require social media platforms to label AI-generated electoral content and implement rapid-response take-down protocols for demonstrably false electoral claims • Establish an independent Electoral Integrity Commission with authority to investigate and sanction platforms that fail to act on verified electoral misinformation • Create a publicly accessible, verified information portal for Canadian election facts, party platforms, and candidate information — maintained by Elections Canada • Fund digital media literacy programs in partnership with schools, libraries, and community organizations, with targeted outreach to seniors and newcomers • Require political advertisers to disclose AI use in campaign materials, with penalties for undisclosed deepfakes or synthetic media CLUSTER 6 — REGIONAL POLICIES Policy That Reaches Every Corner of Canada. Why Regional Matters National policy discussions too often default to urban metrics, urban timelines, and urban infrastructure assumptions. A policy process that ignores geographic diversity — the distances, the time zone differences, the resource-dependent economies, the unique governance challenges of rural, remote, northern, and Indigenous communities — will consistently produce policies that work in Toronto and fail everywhere else. The NWLC is building a standing Regional Policies working group to ensure that women from every region of Canada — not just major urban centres — have a direct pathway into the national policy process. This cluster captures emerging priorities from those communities, with formal resolutions to follow in the next cycle. EMERGING PRIORITY Rural, Remote & Northern Women's Access to Services Regional Policy Working Group — Proposals in Development Women in rural, remote, and northern communities face compounded barriers to healthcare, legal services, shelters, childcare, and economic opportunity. Geographic distance, seasonal road conditions, limited broadband, and under-resourced local governments create a policy gap that affects millions of Canadian women but rarely drives national agenda-setting. PROPOSED DIRECTIONS • Establish a Rural and Remote Women's Services Fund with dedicated allocations for communities below a defined population threshold • Mandate rural and northern impact assessments for all federal social policy proposals — parallel to the existing GBA+ framework • Expand broadband access as a prerequisite for telehealth, remote legal aid, and digital participation in policy processes • Create a Regional Policy Advisory Panel within the NWLC with seats reserved for members from rural, remote, northern, and resource-dependent communities • Develop mobile and seasonal service delivery models for healthcare, legal aid, and shelter access in communities without permanent infrastructure EMERGING PRIORITY Indigenous Women's Leadership in Regional Policy Development Regional Policy Working Group — Proposals in Development Policies that affect Indigenous communities must be developed with Indigenous women at the table — not consulted after the fact. This means dedicated seats in NWLC working groups, co-development of resolutions on issues that intersect with Indigenous rights, and formal protocols for nation-to-nation respectful engagement in the federal policy process. PROPOSED DIRECTIONS • Establish formal co-development protocols between the NWLC and Indigenous women's organizations for resolutions affecting Indigenous communities • Create dedicated funded seats for Indigenous women on NWLC policy working groups at the national level • Align NWLC policy work with UNDRIP implementation and the National Inquiry into MMIWG calls for justice • Support Indigenous-led policy Cafés in remote and northern communities with NWLC facilitation support and translation resources WLC BRITISH COLUMBIA Volunteer Recognition, Incentive and Retention Policy for Vital Canadian Non-Profit Services Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Robin Goldsbury Volunteers are the backbone of Canada's healthcare, cultural, recreational, educational, social, and political life. Yet since 1997, fewer Canadians are volunteering for fewer hours — and the communities hit hardest are women's groups, rural and small-town Canada, and marginalized communities where volunteers sustain services that simply don't exist otherwise. Financial donations earn tax credits; volunteer time and expertise do not. WLC MANITOBA Equitable Co-Ownership in Canadian Mining Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson Canada's mining sector generates enormous wealth — yet the communities most directly affected by extraction, including Indigenous communities and rural residents, are often excluded from meaningful ownership and benefit-sharing. Women in these communities bear the health, environmental, and social impacts while seeing limited economic return. This resolution calls for mandatory co-ownership and benefit-sharing frameworks that ensure Indigenous and affected communities hold genuine stakes in Canadian mining operations. Most of Canada's fertile land (just 7.2% of total land area) sits in southern regions under enormous pressure from housing, infrastructure, and transportation development. Once converted, farmland is almost never recovered. Food security is a women's issue: women make up a growing share of farm operators, and food insecurity falls hardest on women and children. WLC RESOLUTION Regulating the Environmental Impacts of AI Data Centres Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Canada's cooler climate and abundant freshwater make it an increasingly attractive location for AI data centres — but without a federal framework, the rapid growth of this sector risks creating serious environmental harm. AI data centres consume vast amounts of energy and use intensive water-cooling processes that strain both energy grids and freshwater supplies. Indigenous communities near proposed sites bear disproportionate environmental risk. This resolution establishes federal environmental guardrails. KEY ASKS • Require all new AI data centres to meet mandatory renewable energy thresholds as a condition of federal and provincial permitting • Prohibit the use of potable drinking water for data centre cooling; mandate grey water or recycled water systems • Establish a federal environmental assessment framework for large-scale data centre development, including mandatory consultation with affected Indigenous communities • Require public disclosure of energy and water consumption from all federally permitted data centres on an annual basis • Align data centre development policy with Canada's net-zero 2050 commitments and critical freshwater protection obligations WLC RESOLUTION Responsible Use of AI: A Canadian Artificial Intelligence Act Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Artificial intelligence is transforming industries, economies, and daily life — but without a national regulatory framework, Canada risks permitting AI applications that are unsafe, discriminatory, or privacy-invasive. The EU's AI Act provides a model: risk-based regulation that enables innovation while protecting rights. AI systems that make decisions affecting women's employment, healthcare, or justice must be held to the highest standards of fairness and accountability. KEY ASKS • Draft a Canadian Artificial Intelligence Act modelled on the EU AI Act, with risk-based classification of AI applications and sector-specific requirements • Mandate algorithmic impact assessments for high-risk AI systems, including those used in hiring, housing, credit, healthcare, and law enforcement • Apply a reverse onus of proof for AI safety — developers must demonstrate their systems do not discriminate or cause harm before deployment • Require gender, race, and disability disaggregation in AI system testing and audit results • Ensure AI simplifies, rather than complicates, access to government and judicial services for persons with disabilities and multilingual speakers WLC RESOLUTION Returning Humanity and Fairness to the Judicial System Through AI Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission Canada's courts face severe backlogs — delays that deny timely justice and fall hardest on the most vulnerable. At the same time, systemic racism in policing and judicial processes disproportionately impacts Indigenous, Black, and racialized Canadians, including women. This resolution calls for the careful, rights-based integration of AI in the judicial system to reduce backlogs, enhance accuracy, and counteract — not compound — systemic bias. KEY ASKS • Pilot AI-assisted case management systems in federal courts to reduce administrative backlogs and flag scheduling inefficiencies • Require all judicial AI tools to be audited for bias — particularly with respect to race, gender, and socioeconomic status — before and during deployment • Prohibit predictive sentencing or bail algorithms that encode historical discrimination; AI may assist analysis but cannot replace judicial judgment • Fund AI-assisted evidence processing for sexual assault and intimate partner violence cases to reduce forensic backlogs • Establish an independent judicial AI oversight body with civil society, survivor, and racialized community representation CLUSTER 9 — INTERNATIONAL & TRADE PARTNERSHIP Canada as a Partner for Ethical Development. WLC MANITOBA Canada–Sierra Leone Ethical Development and Trade Partnership Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson Canada has a long-standing relationship with Sierra Leone through development partnerships, Canadian-trained professionals, and diaspora ties. The NWLC's policy process is designed to close the gap between good ideas and real change, at every stage from listening to evaluation. Good policies get voted in and stay — but only when the women who drafted them are still in the room when implementation decisions are made. Our process keeps those women in the room. Stage 1 LISTEN Members, data, lived experience Stage 2 SYNTHESIZE Policy Cafés & working groups Stage 3 DEVELOP Draft resolutions & review Stage 4 SUBMIT National policy cycle Stage 5 EVALUATE Track adoption & implementation Timing Designed for Women's Lives Traditional meeting schedules — weekday evenings, in-person only, concentrated in urban centres — have never reflected the full range of women's lives. A policy process that ignores time zone differences, caregiving responsibilities, rural distances, and shift work will systematically exclude the voices it claims to represent. The NWLC is committed to meeting formats that include asynchronous digital participation, evening and weekend options, regional satellite meetings, and childcare-accessible venues as standard practice — not exceptions requiring special requests. From Resolution to Implementation: Closing the Gap The NWLC tracks every resolution that passes through the national policy cycle: Was it adopted? Was it included in platform commitments? Was it implemented after election? Where implementation stalled, we ask why — and we bring that analysis back to the next cycle. This accountability loop is not punitive. It is how we learn, how we hold our own work to a higher standard, and how we ensure that women's priorities are not performative commitments but living policy. These are not formal positions — they are seeds. Café participants proposed a Women's Political Leadership Fund, campaign finance equity rules, and digital safety protocols for women candidates as complementary measures to structural electoral reform. 🌐 Technology, AI & Women's Rights Participants flagged the rapid expansion of AI-generated non-consensual intimate images (NCII) and the use of technology in intimate partner violence and coercive control — stalkerware, GPS tracking misuse, financial account manipulation. Participants proposed a structured NWLC mentorship program pairing emerging policy voices with experienced members, peer networks across provincial commissions, and formal recognition for volunteers who contribute policy work — including letters of recognition for students and young professionals. 🌾 Rural, Remote & Indigenous Women's Priorities Women from rural, remote, and Indigenous communities shared consistent feedback: national policy discussions default to urban metrics, urban timelines, and urban infrastructure assumptions. COLLABORATION & COORDINATION Working Across Commissions Joint Policy Development with the Indigenous Peoples', Seniors', and Youth Commissions Many of the most important policy issues facing women sit at the intersection of multiple commission mandates. Indigenous women's safety and healthcare are not only women's issues — they are Indigenous rights issues. Housing insecurity affects seniors disproportionately. Electoral reform is a youth issue as much as a gender equity issue. The NWLC is committed to formal joint working groups with the Indigenous Peoples' Commission, the Seniors' Commission, and the Youth Commission for policy development on overlapping issues. Joint resolutions, shared research, and co-presented platform asks are all on the table. We are stronger when we work together — and so is the resulting policy. A NOTE FROM THE POLICY CHAIR A Process as Good as the Policies It Produces The resolutions in this cycle — spanning health, reproductive rights, gender-based violence, mental health, housing, governance, technology, natural resources, and international partnerships — represent the work of women across Canada who gave their time, expertise, and often their personal experience to shape policy that matters. They deserve a process that matches the quality of that contribution. Our goal as the NWLC Policy Committee is to be the infrastructure that turns those contributions into change: rigorous, inclusive, accountable, and built for the full range of women's lives. Not just the women who can show up on a Tuesday evening in Ottawa. We look forward to hearing from members. Every comment, correction, and additional idea makes the next cycle stronger.
