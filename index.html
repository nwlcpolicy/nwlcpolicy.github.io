Resolutions, Ideas & Process

Nine issue clusters. One shared vision for women in Canada.
Spring 2026  ·  NWLC Policy Committee  ·  Judith Quinn, NWLC Policy Chair

The National Women's Liberal Commission opens every policy cycle by listening — to members, to data, and to the communities most affected by the decisions made in Ottawa. This edition brings together the resolutions submitted for the 2026 cycle, organized by issue cluster so you can see where our collective energy is focused.

Alongside the formal resolutions, we share the ideas emerging from our Policy Café conversations, and a plain-language overview of how a resolution moves from a single woman's lived experience to a national party position. Good policies get voted in and stay — but only when implementation follows intention. That is the work ahead of us.

Cluster 1 — Health & Reproductive Rights

Rebuilding the System. Protecting the Body.

NWLC Resolution

Achieving Healthcare for All Canadians: A 21st Century Restructuring of the Healthcare System

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission  ·  Contact: Judith Quinn

Canada's healthcare system was designed for a different era. Today, millions of Canadians lack a family doctor, wait times are among the highest in the developed world, and women — who represent the majority of unpaid caregivers and healthcare workers — shoulder the heaviest burden of a system in crisis.

This resolution calls on the Liberal Party to champion a comprehensive restructuring, establishing a Canada Primary Care Act that guarantees every Canadian access to a primary care provider within two years, alongside expanded digital health infrastructure, mental health parity, and long-term care reform.

Key Asks
  • Enact a Canada Primary Care Act guaranteeing access to a primary care provider for all Canadians
  • Invest in digital health infrastructure including national electronic health records and telehealth expansion
  • Establish mental health parity — equal coverage for mental and physical health under public insurance
  • Reform long-term care with national standards, inspections, and a dedicated federal funding stream
  • Increase funding and training for healthcare workers, recognizing women's disproportionate representation in the sector
WLC Alberta

Codifying Reproductive Rights and Access in Canadian Law

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Zuraida Dada

Reproductive rights remain uncodified in Canadian law — a gap that leaves access to abortion and reproductive healthcare vulnerable to political shifts at the provincial level. With legislative attacks on reproductive rights accelerating in neighbouring jurisdictions, this resolution calls for federal codification as a non-negotiable foundation of women's equality.

Key Asks
  • Pass federal legislation codifying the right to abortion and reproductive healthcare access across all provinces and territories
  • Establish national funding floors for reproductive health services that provinces cannot reduce
  • Protect healthcare workers from harassment when providing lawful reproductive services
  • Include contraception and sexual health education as insured services under provincial health plans, with federal incentives
WLC Manitoba

Establishing a Crown Corporation for Pharmaceutical Production and Raw Chemical Synthesis

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed Canada's dangerous dependence on foreign pharmaceutical supply chains. Drug shortages — which disproportionately affect women and children — have become routine. This resolution calls for a Canadian pharmaceutical Crown corporation to manufacture essential medicines domestically, ensuring supply security and reducing costs.

Key Asks
  • Establish a federal Crown corporation for domestic production of essential pharmaceuticals and raw chemical precursors
  • Prioritize production of medications disproportionately needed by women — including hormonal therapies, reproductive health medications, and mental health pharmaceuticals
  • Partner with Canadian universities and biotech sector for research and development
  • Implement a national drug shortage monitoring and response system
WLC British Columbia

Access to Independent Medical Examinations for Victims of Sexual Assaults and Harassment in the Military

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Dr. Maria Omar

Victims of sexual assault and harassment within the Canadian Armed Forces are currently examined by military medical personnel — creating an inherent conflict of interest that undermines survivor trust, compromises forensic evidence, and deters reporting. This resolution calls for independent, civilian-conducted medical examinations as a matter of survivor dignity and justice.

Key Asks
  • Mandate independent civilian medical examinations for all victims of sexual assault and harassment within the Canadian Armed Forces
  • Establish trauma-informed protocols for evidence collection and survivor support, co-developed with survivors
  • Ensure confidentiality and chain of custody for forensic evidence independent of the military chain of command
  • Fund specialized Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) training for civilian practitioners serving military communities
Cluster 2 — Gender-Based Violence & Safety

When the System Fails Women, We Change the System.

"Intimate partner violence kills a woman every six days in Canada. Bailey McCourt was one of them. Her family asked: why did the system let this happen? This resolution is our answer."

— WLC BC, Resolution Preamble, 2026
WLC British Columbia

Advancing Bailey's Law and Reforms to Protect Victims of Intimate Partner Violence

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Dr. Maria Omar

Bailey McCourt was murdered by her estranged husband — released on $500 bail despite prior convictions. Her death, and the deaths of so many women like her, reveals systemic failures in how Canada handles intimate partner violence: bail too low, monitoring too weak, records too fragmented, and femicide not recognized in law as what it is.

This resolution advances "Bailey's Law" — a package of reforms co-developed with Bailey's family, survivors, and Indigenous communities to close these gaps.

Key Asks
  • Bail Reform: Amend the Criminal Code to reverse the onus in intimate partner violence cases — accused must prove they pose no risk, with courts required to weigh prior convictions, stalking, and victim statements
  • GPS Electronic Monitoring: Mandate offender-funded GPS monitoring for high-risk accused, with real-time victim alerts via app — developed with Indigenous and community input
  • National Registry: Create a PIPEDA-compliant, AI-supported database of repeat intimate partner violence offenders, accessible to police and courts
  • Recognize Femicide: Amend the Criminal Code to recognize femicide as a distinct offence; presume first-degree murder when a killing follows documented abuse
  • Allocate $100 million annually for trauma-informed mental health, legal aid, and culturally appropriate shelters
NSWLC — Nova Scotia

Stop Ruthless Cuts to Women and Gender Equality Canada (WAGE)

Sponsor: Nova Scotia Women's Liberal Commission

The 81% cut to WAGE's 2025–2028 budget is not a line-item adjustment — it is the dismantling of the infrastructure that funds shelters, victim support agencies, the National Action Plan on Gender-Based Violence, the 2SLGBTQI+ Secretariat, and the gender budgeting analysis that keeps equality commitments accountable. These cuts reverse decades of progress and renege on a pre-election promise: "We will protect these rights, and fight for true equity for women — in health, in the workplace, and in their access to fundamental rights."

Key Asks
  • Maintain and increase WAGE funding — reject the proposed 81% cut to the 2025–2028 budget
  • Recognize that investing in gender equality is fundamental to nation-building, economic growth, and social stability
  • Honour the National Action Plan to End Gender-Based Violence and all associated funding commitments
  • Protect the 2SLGBTQI+ Secretariat and gender budgeting infrastructure from austerity cuts
  • Uphold Canada's international obligations on gender equality and reject austerity measures that threaten hard-won rights
WLC Alberta

Healing Sexual Violence Survivors — A Tax Credit for Therapy

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Ingrid Dandanell

30–44% of Canadian women experience sexual violence in their lifetime. Healing requires long-term therapy — yet non-medical mental health specialists (counsellors, social workers, psychologists) are not covered under the Canada Health Act. Access to care is determined by ability to pay, forcing many survivors to choose between therapy and basic needs.

Key Asks
  • Implement a non-refundable Sexual Violence Tax Credit for therapy costs — not contingent on police charges, modelled on the Disability Tax Credit
  • Eligibility based on costs for non-medical mental health specialists (counsellors, social workers, psychologists)
  • Apply to all survivors regardless of age; parents may claim for minors
  • Standardize provincial compensation programs — currently ranging from $12,000 (Alberta) to $25,000 (Ontario)
WLC Alberta

Pan-Canadian Framework for Domestic Violence Disclosure Schemes (Clare's Law)

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta

Domestic violence disclosure schemes ("Clare's Law") allow individuals at risk to seek verified information about a partner's history of violence. Uneven provincial adoption has created dangerous gaps — women in some provinces have access to this potentially life-saving information while women in others do not. The federal government can close this gap without infringing on provincial jurisdiction.

Key Asks
  • Establish a Pan-Canadian Framework for Domestic Violence Disclosure, developed with provinces, territories, and Indigenous governments
  • Develop model legislation adaptable by each jurisdiction, with national minimum disclosure standards
  • Provide federally funded training for law enforcement, including standardized RCMP participation
  • Create a secure optional federal digital portal for disclosure requests, improving consistency and data security
  • Ensure cultural safety and equitable access for Indigenous and marginalized communities
NWLC Resolution

Preventing the Misuse of Non-Disclosure Agreements

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Non-disclosure agreements were designed to protect trade secrets — but they have become a tool to silence victims of sexual harassment, discrimination, and abuse. Five times more women than men sign NDAs due to their default use in sexual harassment settlements. A disproportionate number of those signing are racialized or Indigenous. Bill S-232 has begun addressing this; this resolution calls for stronger, broader action.

Key Asks
  • Enact legislation to restrict the misuse of NDAs, aligned with and expanding on Bill S-232
  • Expand prohibited NDA use beyond harassment, discrimination, and violence to include fraud, criminal activity, and gross incompetence
  • Prohibit use of public funds to enter into or litigate NDAs covering misconduct
  • Protect individuals who break NDAs to report serious wrongdoing from civil or criminal liability
WLC Manitoba

Protection of the Rights and Safety of Sex Workers

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson

Criminalisation of sex work contributes to violence, undermines access to justice, and restricts public health services. Indigenous women engaged in sex work face heightened risks due to over-policing and criminal laws. The Canadian Public Health Association and human rights organizations support full decriminalisation as the evidence-based approach to improving safety and dignity.

Key Asks
  • Decriminalise sex work and recognise it as legitimate labour that fulfils human needs for connection and dignity
  • Review and amend federal legislation that inadvertently criminalises sex workers or conflates sex work with trafficking
  • Ensure federal funding does not support programs that equate all sex work with exploitation
  • Develop anti-trafficking initiatives in consultation with sex workers and human rights organizations
  • Implement specific protections for racialized, Indigenous, and migrant sex workers
Cluster 3 — Mental Health & Inclusion

Care That Reaches Every Woman.

WLC Alberta

Advancing Culturally Sensitive Mental Health Care in Canada

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Alberta  ·  Contact: Zuraida Dada

Canada's mental health system was not designed with cultural diversity in mind. Immigrant women, racialized communities, Indigenous peoples, and newcomers face a system that often misdiagnoses, pathologizes cultural expression, or simply lacks practitioners who understand their context. The result: women who most need care go without it, or are harmed by it.

This resolution calls for a national framework for culturally sensitive mental health care — not as an add-on, but as a standard of quality.

Key Asks
  • Develop a National Framework for Culturally Safe Mental Health Care co-designed with racialized, Indigenous, and newcomer communities
  • Fund training requirements in cultural competence and anti-oppression for all federally regulated mental health providers
  • Increase diversity in mental health workforce through targeted scholarships and international credential recognition
  • Establish community mental health hubs in partnership with cultural and faith organizations serving underrepresented communities
  • Require disaggregated data collection on mental health service access by race, gender, immigration status, and disability
NWLC Resolution

Livable, Not Laughable: Transforming the Canada Disability Benefit

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

The Canada Disability Benefit, implemented through Bill C-22, leaves recipients below the poverty line. Benefits tied to family income penalize people with disabilities living with partners or family — undermining financial independence and personal dignity. Women with disabilities face compounded barriers of gender, disability, and inadequate income support.

Key Asks
  • Enhance the Canada Disability Benefit to provide a livable income at or above the Official Poverty Line ($2,000/month in 2025, indexed to inflation)
  • Base eligibility solely on individual circumstances, eliminating family income tests
  • Incorporate regional cost-of-living adjustments — a uniform national amount fails high-cost urban and remote communities equally
  • Introduce a Relocation Tax Credit for persons with disabilities moving closer to essential services
  • Index benefits annually to inflation and account for elevated costs of disability — medical expenses, accessibility aids, specialized equipment
Cluster 4 — Housing

Unlocking Land. Building Homes.

WLC British Columbia

Faith Land Housing Resolution

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Anne Marie Varner

Across Canada, faith communities hold significant parcels of underused or surplus land — land that could house thousands of families. Regulatory barriers, zoning restrictions, and complex partnership frameworks have prevented faith-owned land from contributing to the affordable housing supply, even when faith communities are willing and eager to act.

This resolution calls on the federal government to work with faith communities, municipalities, and non-profits to unlock this land for affordable housing development, particularly for women fleeing violence, single-parent families, seniors, and newcomers.

Key Asks
  • Create a Faith Land–Affordable Housing Partnership Fund to support development on faith-community-owned surplus land
  • Streamline federal incentives and tax treatment for faith organizations that partner with non-profits to build affordable housing
  • Establish a national inventory of faith-owned land with potential for housing development
  • Prioritize units for women fleeing violence, single-parent families, seniors, and newcomers
  • Ensure developments include wraparound supports — childcare, community space, culturally appropriate services
WLC British Columbia

Supporting Canadian Seniors with a Refundable Tax Credit for Relocation

Sponsor: Saanich Gulf Islands EDA & BC Liberal Women's Commission  ·  Contact: Anne Marie Varner

Canada's senior population is projected to reach 25% of the total population by 2030. Sixty percent of seniors delay relocation because moving costs are a financial barrier — trapping them in unsuitable housing far from family, support networks, or adequate care. In rural communities, wait times for senior housing can exceed a decade.

Key Asks
  • Introduce an income-based Seniors Moving Refundable Tax Credit to offset relocation costs for seniors moving more than 100 km for housing, social support, or proximity to family
  • Address the shortage of seniors care in rural communities and improve quality of care homes through increased Canadian investment
  • Transition toward a national not-for-profit seniors housing model
NWLC Resolution

Affordable and Sustainable Housing Model — The Canadian HouseMate Program

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Average home prices have risen over 200% in cities like Toronto and Vancouver since 2010. Homeownership rates for Canadians under 35 have dropped from 55% in 2000 to below 35%. Over 20% of urban properties are owned by corporations or non-resident speculators. This resolution calls for a structural intervention — not just incentives.

Key Asks
  • Establish the Canadian HouseMate Program — CMHC building and selling 100,000 energy-efficient homes annually at 50% below market price, funded by low-interest bonds
  • Limit eligibility to non-property-owning citizens or permanent residents via a transparent lottery, prioritizing families and high-need regions including Indigenous communities
  • Require five-year primary residency, with resales only to eligible non-owners; recapture 15% of profits for future builds
  • Utilize federal, provincial, municipal, and faith-based land for development
  • Address financialization — curb corporate and non-resident speculative ownership of residential properties
Cluster 5 — Governance

A Democracy That Looks Like Canada.

AORRH WLC

Electoral Reform — Move to a Proportional Representation System

Sponsor: WLC AORRH  ·  Contact: Wendy Morihovitis

Under Canada's first-past-the-post system, millions of votes do not elect anyone — and women, Indigenous peoples, and racialized Canadians are consistently underrepresented in legislatures. Proportional representation systems consistently produce more diverse elected bodies, better reflect the range of views held by voters, and reduce the "wasted vote" effect that discourages participation.

This resolution calls on the Liberal Party to revisit its commitment to electoral reform and to champion a proportional or mixed-member system through a citizen-led process — modelled in part on the experiences of New Zealand and Germany.

Key Asks
  • Convene a Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform with gender-balanced, regionally representative, and Indigenous membership
  • Commit to a referendum on proportional representation within the first two years of the next mandate, with the Citizens' Assembly recommendation as the preferred option
  • Adopt gender parity provisions in any new electoral system design
  • Implement automatic voter registration and expand early and online voting to increase participation, particularly among women, youth, and newcomers
NWLC Resolution

Veteran Survivors' Pension — Full Benefits for Military Spouses

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Military spouses follow their partners through a minimum of three postings during a career — relocations that make it impossible to build a pension, accumulate CPP contributions, or maintain continuous employment. When a veteran dies, the spouse who sacrificed her own career for Canada's Armed Forces receives just half the military pension and a fraction of CPP. This resolution closes that gap.

Key Asks
  • Allow surviving veterans' spouses to receive the full CPP and other pension benefits of the veteran for the remainder of the survivor's life
  • Recognize military-spouse career disruption as a qualifying factor in survivor benefit calculations
  • End the reduction of survivor benefits to 50% of the military pension upon the veteran's death
  • Review the 37.5% / 60% CPP survivor split threshold and align it with the economic reality of military family life
NWLC Resolution

Protecting the Public and Our Institutions from Bad Actors — Whistleblower Protection

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Most workers in Canada who reveal wrongdoing have no legal protection and face high risk of retaliation. Current Canadian federal legislation is not fit for purpose — it effectively re-victimizes whistleblowers and protects alleged wrongdoers, lagging far behind other jurisdictions including the European Union's 2019 Whistleblower Protection Directive.

Key Asks
  • Enact comprehensive whistleblower protection legislation modelled on the EU Whistleblower Protection Directive
  • Guarantee competent and impartial due process, with the option to go public if investigations are refused or inadequate
  • Provide immediate, proactive protection from reprisals while allegations are under investigation
  • Require legal assistance paid by the employer for whistleblowers
  • Protect whistleblowers from liability for breaking NDAs or securing evidence of wrongdoing
  • Reverse the onus of proof with respect to retaliation claims
WLC Ontario

Mitigating the Epidemic of Electoral Online Misinformation

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Ontario  ·  Contact: Sumana Gupta

The proliferation of misinformation on social media — amplified by AI — threatens the integrity of Canadian elections. Platforms are often unwilling to mitigate it. Women candidates and candidates from marginalized communities are disproportionately targeted. This resolution calls for a non-partisan federal response.

Key Asks
  • Create a non-partisan, publicly accessible platform that verifies political information and mitigates the effects of electoral misinformation
  • Identify and sanction individuals intentionally spreading misinformation about candidates — including false claims about citizenship, birthplace, education, and qualifications
  • Prohibit identity impersonation of the Chief Electoral Officer, election officers, Members of Parliament, candidates, and party leaders
  • Enact federal legislation modelled on Quebec's Bill 98 — financial and legal sanctions for purposeful spreading of electoral misinformation
Cluster 6 — Regional Policies

Policy That Reaches Every Corner of Canada.

Why Regional Matters

National policy discussions too often default to urban metrics, urban timelines, and urban infrastructure assumptions. A policy process that ignores geographic diversity — the distances, the time zone differences, the resource-dependent economies, the unique governance challenges of rural, remote, northern, and Indigenous communities — will consistently produce policies that work in Toronto and fail everywhere else.

The NWLC is building a standing Regional Policies working group to ensure that women from every region of Canada — not just major urban centres — have a direct pathway into the national policy process. This cluster captures emerging priorities from those communities, with formal resolutions to follow in the next cycle.

Emerging Priority

Rural, Remote & Northern Women's Access to Services

Regional Policy Working Group — Proposals in Development

Women in rural, remote, and northern communities face compounded barriers to healthcare, legal services, shelters, childcare, and economic opportunity. Geographic distance, seasonal road conditions, limited broadband, and under-resourced local governments create a policy gap that affects millions of Canadian women but rarely drives national agenda-setting.

Proposed Directions
  • Establish a Rural and Remote Women's Services Fund with dedicated allocations for communities below a defined population threshold
  • Mandate rural and northern impact assessments for all federal social policy proposals — parallel to the existing GBA+ framework
  • Expand broadband access as a prerequisite for telehealth, remote legal aid, and digital participation in policy processes
  • Create a Regional Policy Advisory Panel within the NWLC with seats reserved for members from rural, remote, northern, and resource-dependent communities
  • Develop mobile and seasonal service delivery models for healthcare, legal aid, and shelter access in communities without permanent infrastructure
Emerging Priority

Indigenous Women's Leadership in Regional Policy Development

Regional Policy Working Group — Proposals in Development

Policies that affect Indigenous communities must be developed with Indigenous women at the table — not consulted after the fact. This means dedicated seats in NWLC working groups, co-development of resolutions on issues that intersect with Indigenous rights, and formal protocols for nation-to-nation respectful engagement in the federal policy process.

Proposed Directions
  • Establish formal co-development protocols between the NWLC and Indigenous women's organizations for resolutions affecting Indigenous communities
  • Create dedicated funded seats for Indigenous women on NWLC policy working groups at the national level
  • Align NWLC policy work with UNDRIP implementation and the National Inquiry into MMIWG calls for justice
  • Support Indigenous-led policy Cafés in remote and northern communities with NWLC facilitation support and translation resources
WLC British Columbia

Volunteer Recognition, Incentive and Retention Policy for Vital Canadian Non-Profit Services

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia  ·  Contact: Robin Goldsbury

Volunteers are the backbone of Canada's healthcare, cultural, recreational, educational, social, and political life. Yet since 1997, fewer Canadians are volunteering for fewer hours — and the communities hit hardest are women's groups, rural and small-town Canada, and marginalized communities where volunteers sustain services that simply don't exist otherwise. Financial donations earn tax credits; volunteer time and expertise do not.

Key Asks
  • Develop a National Volunteer Recognition, Incentive and Retention Strategy to support vital non-profit and community services
  • Introduce a Volunteer Tax Credit — for example, 150 hours at a $20/hour notional value could generate a $900 Donation Tax Credit claim (30%)
  • Expand data collection on volunteerism through the long-form census to understand engagement patterns, barriers, and policy opportunities
  • Prioritize equity in volunteer support, with heightened focus on women's groups, Indigenous and minority communities, and rural Canada where volunteers sustain services unavailable elsewhere
  • Explore micro-credentials, youth and senior recognition programs, and federal support for volunteer management infrastructure
Cluster 7 — Natural Resources & Economy

Equitable Ownership of Canada's Wealth.

WLC Manitoba

Equitable Co-Ownership in Canadian Mining

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson

Canada's mining sector generates enormous wealth — yet the communities most directly affected by extraction, including Indigenous communities and rural residents, are often excluded from meaningful ownership and benefit-sharing. Women in these communities bear the health, environmental, and social impacts while seeing limited economic return.

This resolution calls for mandatory co-ownership and benefit-sharing frameworks that ensure Indigenous and affected communities hold genuine stakes in Canadian mining operations.

Key Asks
  • Require minimum co-ownership thresholds for Indigenous communities in mining operations on or near traditional territories
  • Establish federal benefit-sharing agreements as a condition of federal mining licences on Crown land
  • Create a Women in Mining Equity Fund supporting Indigenous women and women in affected communities to participate in ownership and governance
  • Mandate environmental and social impact assessments that include gender-disaggregated data and women's participation
WLC Ontario

Protection of Canada's Farmland

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Ontario

Canada's fertile farmland represents only 7.2% of total land — and it is shrinking. The reported amount of farmland has dropped 8% in two decades, from 68 million hectares in 2001 to 62 million in 2021, while Canada's population continues to grow. Conversion of farmland to housing, infrastructure, and transportation is often irreversible. Food security is a national security issue — and women farmers are disproportionately affected by the lack of support and protection.

Key Asks
  • Protect Canada's farmland as an issue of national security related to food security
  • Incentivize sustainable farming practices and provide farmer supports to maintain production for Canadians and international markets
  • Continue and expand support for women in farming through programs such as the Agri-Mentor program and FCC Women Entrepreneur Program
  • Develop federal policy to prevent irreversible conversion of agricultural land to non-farm uses
Cluster 8 — International & Trade Partnership

Canada as a Partner for Ethical Development.

WLC Manitoba

Canada–Sierra Leone Ethical Development and Trade Partnership

Sponsor: Women's Liberal Commission Manitoba  ·  Contact: Anne Thompson

Canada has a long-standing relationship with Sierra Leone through development partnerships, Canadian-trained professionals, and diaspora ties. A formal bilateral trade and development framework — built on ethical standards, gender equity requirements, and mutual benefit — would deepen this relationship while modelling a new approach to Global South partnerships.

Key Asks
  • Negotiate a Canada–Sierra Leone Ethical Development and Trade Framework anchored in gender equity, environmental sustainability, and mutual benefit
  • Include mandatory gender impact assessments in all trade and investment agreements
  • Strengthen diaspora engagement mechanisms to channel Sierra Leonean-Canadian expertise into development programming
  • Tie trade preferences to measurable improvements in women's rights, healthcare access, and girls' education outcomes in Sierra Leone
Cluster 9 — Technology & AI

Governing the Tools That Are Changing Everything.

NWLC Resolution

Responsible Use of AI — A Canadian Artificial Intelligence Act

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Artificial intelligence is transforming industries, economies, and societies — but without a regulatory framework, it poses growing risks to privacy, fairness, and safety. The EU's AI Act provides a proven risk-based model. Canada needs its own legislation, designed to protect the most vulnerable while enabling innovation.

Key Asks
  • Draft a Canadian Artificial Intelligence Act (CAIA) modelled on the EU AI Act, with mandatory risk-based regulation
  • High-risk AI (healthcare, policing, judicial systems) requires developers to prove safety and fairness before deployment
  • Establish a Canadian AI Commission with provincial representation and a scientific panel for transparency and accountability
  • Ban unacceptable AI uses including social scoring and limit government AI use with public reporting requirements
  • Deploy AI assistants for government services that support multilingual access and disability accommodations
  • Timeline: CAIA drafted by 2026, Commission established by 2027, full implementation by 2028
NWLC Resolution

Returning Humanity and Fairness to the Judicial System Through AI

Sponsor: National Women's Liberal Commission

Canada's judicial system faces severe backlogs, systemic racism in policing and judicial processes, and barriers that prevent marginalized communities — including women and Indigenous peoples — from accessing justice. AI, implemented with human oversight and equity safeguards, can help address these failures while improving access for those who cannot afford legal representation.

Key Asks
  • Implement secure online portals for evidence and affidavit management and AI-driven assistance for forms and self-represented litigants
  • Deploy AI for automated backlog reduction and streamlined case processing — with human judges overseeing trials and final decisions
  • Use AI for bias-free preliminary assessments to correct disparities in judicial outcomes affecting racialized and marginalized communities
  • Improve AI-driven tools to address technology-facilitated gender-based violence and enhance justice for marginalized women
  • Align with the Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA) to ensure fairness and transparency in policing
How We Work

The NWLC Policy Process: From Lived Experience to Party Position

Why Process Matters as Much as Policy

A resolution that no one implements is not a policy — it's a document. The NWLC's policy process is designed to close the gap between good ideas and real change, at every stage from listening to evaluation.

Good policies get voted in and stay — but only when the women who drafted them are still in the room when implementation decisions are made. Our process keeps those women in the room.

Timing Designed for Women's Lives

Traditional meeting schedules — weekday evenings, in-person only, concentrated in urban centres — have never reflected the full range of women's lives. A policy process that ignores time zone differences, caregiving responsibilities, rural distances, and shift work will systematically exclude the voices it claims to represent.

The NWLC is committed to meeting formats that include asynchronous digital participation, evening and weekend options, regional satellite meetings, and childcare-accessible venues as standard practice — not exceptions requiring special requests.

From Resolution to Implementation: Closing the Gap

The NWLC tracks every resolution that passes through the national policy cycle: Was it adopted? Was it included in platform commitments? Was it implemented after election? Where implementation stalled, we ask why — and we bring that analysis back to the next cycle.

This accountability loop is not punitive. It is how we learn, how we hold our own work to a higher standard, and how we ensure that women's priorities are not performative commitments but living policy.

Policy Café — Emerging Ideas

Conversations Worth Continuing

The Policy Café format brings women together in facilitated small-group conversations to surface ideas before they become resolutions. These are not formal positions — they are seeds. We share them here to invite further input from NWLC members across the country.

💊 Pharmacare & Essential Medicines

Several Café conversations surfaced strong interest in a universal pharmacare program anchored to the proposed Crown corporation pharmaceutical production model — reducing costs, eliminating coverage gaps, and ending the reality of women choosing between medication and food. The intersection of pharmaceutical sovereignty and pharmacare is an area where the NWLC can bring a distinctive voice.

🏠 Women & Housing Security

Beyond the Faith Land resolution, Café participants raised the persistent issue of women's housing insecurity following relationship breakdown, particularly for older women and newcomers with limited credit history. Ideas included a federal rental deposit loan program, fast-track housing for women fleeing violence, and zoning incentives for mixed-income developments that include transitional housing units.

🗳️ Women in Elected Office

The electoral reform resolution sparked parallel conversations about the barriers women face as candidates — harassment, financial barriers to campaigning, and the gendered nature of media coverage. Café participants proposed a Women's Political Leadership Fund, campaign finance equity rules, and digital safety protocols for women candidates as complementary measures to structural electoral reform.

🌐 Technology, AI & Women's Rights

Participants flagged the rapid expansion of AI-generated non-consensual intimate images (NCII) and the use of technology in intimate partner violence and coercive control — stalkerware, GPS tracking misuse, financial account manipulation. A resolution package addressing digital safety, AI regulation, and online gender-based violence was identified as a priority for the next cycle.

🤝 Mentorship & Next-Generation Policy Leaders

A recurring Café theme: brilliant policy ideas from women who are newer to the process don't always find their way forward — not because the ideas aren't good, but because the pathways aren't clear. Participants proposed a structured NWLC mentorship program pairing emerging policy voices with experienced members, peer networks across provincial commissions, and formal recognition for volunteers who contribute policy work — including letters of recognition for students and young professionals.

🌾 Rural, Remote & Indigenous Women's Priorities

Women from rural, remote, and Indigenous communities shared consistent feedback: national policy discussions default to urban metrics, urban timelines, and urban infrastructure assumptions. A standing NWLC working group on rural and Indigenous women's policy — with direct input from regional communities — was proposed as a structural solution, not just a one-time initiative.

Collaboration & Coordination

Working Across Commissions

Joint Policy Development with the Indigenous Peoples', Seniors', and Youth Commissions

Many of the most important policy issues facing women sit at the intersection of multiple commission mandates. Indigenous women's safety and healthcare are not only women's issues — they are Indigenous rights issues. Housing insecurity affects seniors disproportionately. Electoral reform is a youth issue as much as a gender equity issue.

The NWLC is committed to formal joint working groups with the Indigenous Peoples' Commission, the Seniors' Commission, and the Youth Commission for policy development on overlapping issues. Joint resolutions, shared research, and co-presented platform asks are all on the table. We are stronger when we work together — and so is the resulting policy.

A Note from the Policy Chair

A Process as Good as the Policies It Produces

The twenty-five resolutions in this cycle represent the work of women across Canada who gave their time, expertise, and often their personal experience to shape policy that matters. They deserve a process that matches the quality of that contribution.

Our goal as the NWLC Policy Committee is to be the infrastructure that turns those contributions into change: rigorous, inclusive, accountable, and built for the full range of women's lives. Not just the women who can show up on a Tuesday evening in Ottawa.

We look forward to hearing from members. Every comment, correction, and additional idea makes the next cycle stronger.

Dr. Maria Omar
Policy Chair, Women's Liberal Commission British Columbia
